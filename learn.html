Learn About Exoplanets

Discover the fascinating world of planets beyond our solar system and the methods used to detect them.

What Are Exoplanets?

Exoplanets, or extrasolar planets, are planets that orbit stars outside our solar system. Since the first confirmed detection in 1995, astronomers have discovered thousands of these distant worlds, revolutionizing our understanding of planetary systems and the potential for life beyond Earth.

Types of Exoplanets

Rocky Planets

Similar to Earth and Mars, these planets have solid surfaces and are typically smaller than gas giants.

Gas Giants

Large planets composed mainly of hydrogen and helium, similar to Jupiter and Saturn.

Super-Earths

Rocky planets larger than Earth but smaller than Neptune, with no equivalent in our solar system.

Hot Jupiters

Gas giants that orbit very close to their host stars, resulting in extremely high temperatures.

Why Exoplanets Matter

  • Help us understand planetary formation and evolution
  • Search for potentially habitable worlds
  • Quest to find signs of life beyond Earth
  • Advance our knowledge of stellar systems

The Transit Method

The most successful technique for discovering exoplanets

How It Works

Monitor Star Brightness

Telescopes continuously measure the brightness of stars over time.

Detect Brightness Dips

3

Analyze the Pattern

Regular, periodic dips indicate the presence of an orbiting planet.

4

Calculate Properties

The depth and duration of the dip reveal the planet's size and orbital characteristics.

Data Variables Glossary

Understanding the key measurements used in exoplanet detection

Orbital Period

Definition: The time it takes for a planet to complete one full orbit around its host star.

Units: Days, hours, or years

Example: Earth's orbital period is 365.25 days, while some hot Jupiters have periods of just a few days.

Significance: Shorter periods indicate planets closer to their stars, which affects temperature and atmospheric conditions.

Transit Duration

Definition: The time it takes for a planet to completely cross in front of its host star during a transit event.

Units: Hours or minutes

Example: A typical transit might last 2-6 hours, depending on the planet's size and orbital speed.

Significance: Combined with orbital period, helps determine the planet's distance from its star and the star's size.

Planetary Radius

Definition: The size of the planet, typically measured relative to Earth's radius (R⊕).

Units: Earth radii (R⊕) or kilometers

Example: Jupiter has a radius of about 11 R⊕, while some exoplanets are smaller than Earth at 0.5 R⊕.

Transit Depth

Definition: The percentage decrease in a star's brightness when a planet transits in front of it.

Units: Percentage (%) or parts per million (ppm)

Example: Earth would cause a 0.008% (80 ppm) dip in the Sun's brightness, while larger planets cause deeper dips.
