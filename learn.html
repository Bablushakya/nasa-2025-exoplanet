Learn About Exoplanets

Discover the fascinating world of planets beyond our solar system and learn how we detect them using cutting-edge technology and AI.

What Are Exoplanets?

Exoplanets, or extrasolar planets, are planets that orbit stars outside our solar system. Since the first confirmed detection in 1995, astronomers have discovered thousands of these distant worlds.

Why Are They Important?

  • Help us understand planetary formation and evolution
  • Search for potentially habitable worlds
  • Expand our knowledge of the universe
  • Answer the question: "Are we alone?"

The Transit Method

The transit method is the most successful technique for detecting exoplanets. It works by measuring the tiny decrease in a star's brightness when a planet passes in front of it.

How It Works:

  1. Observation: Continuously monitor star brightness
  2. Detection: Look for periodic dimming events
  3. Analysis: Measure the depth and duration of transits
  4. Confirmation: Verify the planetary nature of the signal

Key Data Variables

Orbital Period

Definition: The time it takes for a planet to complete one orbit around its host star.

Measurement: Measured in days, from the time between successive transits.

Significance: Shorter periods indicate planets closer to their stars, which are typically hotter.

Example: Earth's orbital period is 365.25 days, while some hot Jupiters have periods of just a few days.

Transit Duration

Definition: The time it takes for a planet to completely cross the face of its host star.

Measurement: Measured in hours, from first contact to last contact.

Significance: Depends on the planet's orbital speed and the star's size.

Example: Earth's transit duration as seen from a distant star would be about 13 hours.

Planetary Radius

Definition: The size of the planet, typically measured relative to Earth's radius.

Measurement: Derived from the transit depth and the star's known radius.

Significance: Helps classify planets (Earth-like, super-Earth, Neptune-like, Jupiter-like).

Example: Jupiter has a radius about 11 times that of Earth.

Transit Depth

Definition: The percentage decrease in starlight during a transit.

Measurement: Calculated as the ratio of planet area to star area.

Significance: Directly related to the planet's size - larger planets create deeper transits.

Example: Earth would cause a 0.008% decrease in the Sun's brightness.

Types of Exoplanets

Rocky Planets

Similar to Earth and Mars, these planets have solid surfaces and are typically smaller than Neptune.

Size: 0.5 - 2 Earth radii

Super-Earths

Larger than Earth but smaller than Neptune, these planets may have thick atmospheres or be rocky.

Size: 1.25 - 2 Earth radii

Neptune-like

Gas planets with thick atmospheres, similar in size to Neptune in our solar system.

Size: 2 - 6 Earth radii

Gas Giants

Large planets composed mainly of hydrogen and helium, similar to Jupiter and Saturn.

Size: 6+ Earth radii
